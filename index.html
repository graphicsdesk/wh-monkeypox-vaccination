Medical experts say stigma, insufficient educational outreach cause racial disparities in monkeypox vaccination

By Esther Sun and Sophie Dau

September 20, 2022

Photo by Kathy Fang

Four months after the first COVID-19 vaccine was approved by the Food and Drug Administration last year, West Harlem’s Community Board 9 still did not have a single COVID-19 vaccination site, despite former Mayor Bill De Blasio’s promise to prioritize neighborhoods that have historically suffered from racial inequity. At that time, there were two sites on the Upper West Side and three on the Upper East Side.

Now, West Harlem residents live in proximity to three monkeypox vaccination sites—one run by Ryan Health in Central Harlem, one by the New York City Department of Health and Mental Hygiene in Central Harlem, and one by Ryan Health in Washington Heights. There are three sites downtown as well.

While the current geographic distribution of monkeypox vaccination sites is more balanced than the initial distribution of COVID-19 sites, vaccination rates still show a disparity across racial lines. Many more white individuals have received vaccines than Black and Hispanic individuals, despite contracting fewer cases of monkeypox. To explain this disparity, medical professionals cite persistent issues of community distrust and insufficient access to information—both of which also contributed to COVID-19 vaccination disparities.

One major roadblock to community vaccination is the perceived stigma around the disease. Though monkeypox has not been labeled a sexually transmitted disease, the Department of Health is prioritizing vaccinating gay men, bisexual men, and men who have sex with other men, as they are at higher risk for the disease. When receiving the vaccine, individuals must fill out questionnaires confirming their sexuality and that they have had sex with multiple anonymous partners in the past two weeks.

Dr. Barbara Warren, senior director of LGBT programs and policies at the Mount Sinai Health System, believes these requirements cause some eligible residents to avoid the vaccine. Warren serves as part of the monkeypox vaccine collaboration run by the Department of Health.

“If you’re a person of color, and you also identify as gay or bisexual, or gender-diverse, you’re dealing with multiple stigmas and discriminations that people have experienced and anticipate,” she said. “That might be off-putting to some people.”

However, Warren does believe this barrier is decreasing. The city has started to provide monkeypox vaccines at clinics and community centers that serve as general vaccination sites, including for the COVID-19 vaccine. This shift has allowed individuals to get the vaccine “without necessarily even having other people know what they’re there for.”

Another issue for access was the limited amount of vaccines the city received. With only 1,000 doses originally, many clinics faced seemingly endless lines and had to turn many away. Warren believes the city handled early vaccination efforts the best it could under the circumstances.

“They had a limited supply, and they had to ramp up producing this, producing enough vaccine, and they really had to focus in on the most vulnerable communities for this,” she said. “Given all the challenges that they had, they did a pretty good job of rolling it out as fast as they could.”

Others saw the original rollout as mismanaged and panic-inducing. Alexander Borsa, who studies sexual and reproductive health at the Mailman School of Public Health and serves as part of the HIV Planning Group at the Department of Health, viewed the monkeypox vaccine rollout as poorly planned, inciting fear among at-risk communities without the proper resources.

“Especially in those early days, when they say gay or bisexual men who have sex with men in the city, some big category of people are eligible for this vaccine,” Borsa said. “They know they only have 1,000 doses, and they know there’s going to be this huge swarm of people who are now activated and worried and want to know more information but can’t access the vaccine, are now afraid of that, and don’t really have a lot of other information available.”

Even a few months later, Borsa said the Department of Health’s official information was inaccessible and not easily digestible. He believes many individuals are relying on community-spread information, which has caused a lag because community information travels more slowly.

Though the city has seen 2,023 monkeypox cases among Black and Hispanic individuals and 781 among white individuals as of Sept. 18, only 34,603 Black and Hispanic individuals have been vaccinated compared to 45,576 white individuals.

While data is not available for neighborhood-specific vaccination rates, West Harlem’s primarily Black and Latinx makeup—22 percent and 38 percent, respectively—means this disparity likely affects the neighborhood. This trend reflects previous health crises, most notably COVID-19, where West Harlem did not have the same access to city-wide resources as other boroughs.

Despite the initial disparities, however, the vaccine has become more equitably distributed across racial demographics. In recent meetings with the Department of Health over the past few weeks, Warren has seen the gap in vaccination between white and Black individuals become smaller and the rates of infection more generally decrease city-wide.

Borsa, too, believes the resources and the vaccine itself are now more accessible.

“It’s important to acknowledge and celebrate the good work that’s been done, as well as always push our governmental bodies further to identify mistakes that maybe weren’t needing to be made,” he said.

Similarly, Warren advocates for continued support from a public health perspective, emphasizing strong communication to the public regarding the vaccine.

“I really think that people need to be reassured, educated, encouraged, and not stigmatized, and given really free and affordable access to prevention and the whole vaccine,” she said. “We were just a part of that.”
